# Fund Your Gateway

On-chain gateway operators must maintain a funded ETH account on Arbitrum One before routing any jobs. This page walks you through acquiring ETH, bridging it to Arbitrum, and depositing it into the `TicketBroker` contract that backs your gateway's payment tickets.

<Warning>
  **This page applies to Video Gateways and Dual Gateways only.** If you are running an AI Gateway in off-chain mode, you do not need ETH in the gateway process. Off-chain gateways and clearinghouse-backed gateways skip this step entirely. Not sure which applies to you? See [Payment Paths](/v2/gateways/guides/payments/payment-paths).
</Warning>

---

## What you will need

Before starting, confirm you have the following:

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| ETH on Arbitrum One | Not Ethereum mainnet. Livepeer's protocol operates on Arbitrum One. |
| Your gateway's ETH address | Created automatically when you first ran `go-livepeer`. Found in your keystore. |
| An Arbitrum RPC endpoint | Public endpoints exist; your own is more reliable for production. |
| A running gateway with `livepeer_cli` access | Default CLI port is `5935`. |

## Step 1 — Get ETH on Arbitrum One

Livepeer's `TicketBroker` contract lives on Arbitrum One (not Ethereum mainnet). You need ETH on that network specifically. There are three ways to get it there.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Already have ETH on Arbitrum">
    If you already hold ETH in an Arbitrum One wallet, simply transfer the required amount to your gateway's ETH address. No bridging is needed.

    Your gateway address is the public key of the keystore created when you first ran `go-livepeer`. Check your `~/.lpData/keystore` directory or review your startup logs for the address.
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Have ETH on Ethereum mainnet">
    You need to bridge ETH from Ethereum mainnet to Arbitrum One. Use the official Arbitrum bridge:

    **Bridge:** [bridge.arbitrum.io](https://bridge.arbitrum.io/)

    The bridging process involves two transactions: an approval and a deposit. Allow 10 to 20 minutes for the deposit to finalise on Arbitrum. If the bridge fails due to gas price volatility, Arbitrum provides a retry panel at [retryable-tx-panel.arbitrum.io](https://retryable-tx-panel.arbitrum.io/).

    <Note>
      ETH bridges straightforwardly via the Arbitrum bridge. You do not need to do anything special for native ETH; only ERC-20 tokens like LPT require the token address import step.
    </Note>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="No ETH yet">
    Some centralised exchanges support direct withdrawals to Arbitrum One, which avoids the bridging step entirely. You receive ETH directly in your Arbitrum wallet.

    For a current list of exchanges supporting Arbitrum One, see the [Arbitrum Exchange Reference](/v2/gateways/references/arbitrum-exchanges) in the Resources section. This list is dynamically sourced from CoinGecko.

    <Note>
      Livepeer does not recommend any specific exchange. Verify network support before initiating a withdrawal.
    </Note>
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Step 2 — Transfer ETH to your gateway address

Once you have ETH on Arbitrum One, send it to your gateway's Ethereum address. This address is the keystore account created during gateway installation.

If you are unsure of your gateway address, run `livepeer_cli` and select **Option 1: Get node status**. Your address will appear under **BROADCASTER STATS**.

---

## Step 3 — Deposit and set your reserve

Livepeer's payment system splits your gateway funds into two components. Understanding both is important before you set the amounts.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="What is the deposit?">
    The **deposit** is the primary balance from which orchestrators are paid when they redeem winning PM tickets. As your gateway sends tickets across jobs, winning tickets are redeemed against this deposit. When the deposit runs too low, orchestrators begin rejecting your tickets and jobs fail.

    Think of the deposit as your running payment balance: it is depleted by usage and needs to be topped up periodically.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="What is the reserve?">
    The **reserve** is a collateral backstop that protects orchestrators against gateway non-payment. It is not consumed directly by ticket redemption under normal operation; rather, it is available as a protection mechanism for orchestrators with a stake-based relationship to your gateway.

    The reserve also determines the face value of your PM tickets. Tickets are sized as a fraction of the reserve, so a larger reserve allows for higher-value tickets per redemption event.

    <Warning>
      For production environments, Livepeer recommends a reserve of at least **0.36 ETH** to prevent service interruptions during gas price spikes. [Issue #3744](https://github.com/livepeer/go-livepeer/issues/3744) tracks work to reduce this requirement in future releases.
    </Warning>
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open livepeer_cli">
    On the host running your gateway:

    ```bash
    livepeer_cli -host 127.0.0.1 -http 5935
    ```

    You will see the interactive CLI menu.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Select Option 11 — Invoke deposit broadcasting funds">
    Type `11` and press Enter. The CLI will prompt you for two amounts.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Enter deposit and reserve amounts">
    Enter the amounts in ETH when prompted. Use these values as a starting point:

    | Purpose | Recommended amount | Notes |
    |---|---|---|
    | **Testing only** | 0.065 ETH deposit + 0.03 ETH reserve | Minimum functional setup. Not for production. |
    | **Production** | 0.1 ETH+ deposit + 0.36 ETH reserve | Reserve covers gas spike scenarios. |

    The ticket face value is calculated as a fraction of your reserve. In the testing example: `0.03 ETH reserve ÷ 100 = 0.0003 ETH per ticket`.

    <Note>
      These amounts are in ETH, not wei. The CLI accepts decimal ETH values directly.
    </Note>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Confirm the transaction">
    The CLI will prompt for confirmation. Review the amounts, then confirm. The transaction is submitted to the `TicketBroker` contract on Arbitrum One. Allow a few seconds for confirmation.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Verify your balance">
    Select **Option 1: Get node status** in `livepeer_cli`. Scroll to the **BROADCASTER STATS** section. You should see your deposit and reserve amounts reflected there.

    You can also verify on-chain via [Livepeer Explorer](https://explorer.livepeer.org) by searching for your gateway address.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Monitoring and topping up

Your deposit depletes with every winning ticket redeemed. The reserve normally stays intact unless there is a default event. You need to monitor and top up your deposit regularly.

<Warning>
  When your deposit falls below a threshold, orchestrators will begin rejecting your payment tickets. Jobs will silently fail or stall. Monitor your deposit balance proactively rather than reactively.
</Warning>

**Check your balance:**

```bash
livepeer_cli -host 127.0.0.1 -http 5935
# Select Option 1 → review BROADCASTER STATS
```

**Top up your deposit:** Repeat Steps 3 to 5 above. You can top up at any time by invoking Option 11 again and adding further ETH.

**Automate monitoring:** For production deployments, monitor your gateway's ETH balance via your Arbitrum RPC endpoint. Alert when the deposit falls below your minimum operating threshold.

## Unlock and withdraw

If you need to withdraw funds from your gateway's `TicketBroker` deposit, there is an unlock period. This prevents instantaneous withdrawal attacks.

{/* REVIEW: Confirm the current TicketBroker unlock period (historically 7 days on L1, may differ on Arbitrum). Rick to verify the current on-chain value. */}

Use `livepeer_cli` to initiate an unlock request. After the unlock period expires, you can withdraw the funds to your Ethereum address.

<Tip>
  If you are winding down a gateway, initiate the unlock well in advance. The unlock period protects orchestrators who may have pending tickets against your deposit.
</Tip>
