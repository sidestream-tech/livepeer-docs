Running a gateway that routes your own traffic is one thing. Running a gateway that other developers and applications can discover and use is another. This guide covers how to make your gateway reachable and findable, and is honest about what formal discoverability mechanisms exist today versus what is planned.

<Warning>
Unlike orchestrators, which register on-chain via the ServiceRegistry contract and appear in the Livepeer Explorer automatically, there is no equivalent on-chain registry for gateways. Discoverability today is manual and community-driven. This page documents what currently exists and how to use it.
</Warning>

---

## Service URI configuration

The first requirement is that your gateway is reachable at a stable, public address.

```bash
-serviceAddr your-gateway.example.com:8935
```

`-serviceAddr` tells the gateway what address to advertise to the components that connect to it. For a public gateway, this must be your externally resolvable DNS name and port, not `localhost` or an internal IP.

For the external hostname used in responses:

```bash
-gatewayHost your-gateway.example.com
```

### DNS setup

Point a dedicated subdomain at your gateway server's public IP. A dedicated subdomain (rather than an IP address) lets you change servers without updating your customers' integrations.

```
ai-gateway.yourdomain.com.   A   <your-server-ip>
```

For multi-region deployments, use GeoDNS or a load balancer DNS record that routes to the nearest region.

### HTTPS is required

External clients must reach your gateway over HTTPS. Set up TLS termination via a reverse proxy before exposing the service. See [Production Hardening](/v2/gateways/guides/advanced-operations/production-hardening) for Caddy and Nginx examples.

## Advertising your capabilities

Developers choosing a gateway need to know what yours supports. There is no standard machine-readable format for this yet, but publishing a clear capabilities document on your domain is the current best practice.

Document these things:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Supported workloads">
    State explicitly whether your gateway handles:
    - AI inference (and which pipelines: text-to-image, image-to-video, LLM, etc.)
    - Video transcoding
    - Live AI (video-to-video)
    - BYOC pipelines

    If you support AI inference, list the models available via your connected orchestrators. You can generate this dynamically from your gateway's `/getNetworkCapabilities` endpoint:

    ```bash
    curl https://your-gateway.example.com/getNetworkCapabilities | \
      jq '[.orchestrators[].capabilities_prices[].model_id] | unique'
    ```
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Pricing">
    Publish your customer-facing pricing clearly. Remember that your gateway has two pricing layers:

    - What you pay orchestrators (`-maxPricePerCapability`, `-maxPricePerUnit`) — this is your cost floor
    - What you charge customers — this is your business decision

    These do not need to match. Most public gateways add a margin and present pricing in human-readable units (per image, per minute of video) rather than in wei per pixel.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Authentication requirements">
    Describe how developers authenticate with your gateway: API key, OAuth, or open access. If you require registration, link to your signup page.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Rate limits and SLAs">
    Document any rate limits and what SLA you offer. Even a best-effort statement ("we target 99% uptime") sets expectations and builds trust.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Current discovery methods

These are the mechanisms developers use today to find public gateways. Use as many as are appropriate for your intended audience.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Community channels">
    The most effective current method. Announce your gateway in the [Livepeer Discord](https://discord.gg/livepeer) in the `#local-gateways` channel. Include:

    - Your gateway endpoint URL
    - Supported pipelines and models
    - Pricing and any access requirements
    - Your operating region(s)

    The Forum at [forum.livepeer.org](https://forum.livepeer.org) is appropriate for longer announcements with more detail, and threads are indexed by search engines.

    Keep your announcement updated as your capabilities change.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Livepeer Tools">
    [tools.livepeer.cloud](https://tools.livepeer.cloud) is a community-maintained dashboard that tracks active network participants. Contact the maintainers to have your gateway listed.

    {/* REVIEW: Confirm whether tools.livepeer.cloud lists gateways (not just orchestrators) and how to request a listing — community verification needed */}
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Gateway providers directory">
    The Foundation maintains a list of known public gateways in the [Gateway Providers](/v2/gateways/guides/operator-considerations/commercial-gateways) page (now in the Developers tab). Contact the Foundation to have your gateway added.

    SPE-funded gateways (Livepeer Cloud, Daydream) are listed automatically as part of their SPE agreement.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Direct integration">
    For developers building applications, the most common path is direct integration: they find your gateway through one of the channels above and hardcode your endpoint. Make it easy for them by providing:

    - A clear API reference (your gateway endpoint + the standard [AI API reference](/v2/gateways/resources/technical/api-reference/AI-API/ai))
    - A working quickstart curl command
    - A stable versioned endpoint URL
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Connecting to the Livepeer marketplace

The Livepeer Marketplace is a programmatic interface for orchestrators to advertise compute and for gateways to discover and route to that compute. It is not a discovery mechanism for applications to find gateways.

As a gateway operator, your relationship to the marketplace is as a **broker**: you query it to find orchestrators, not to list yourself for applications to find. The marketplace is upstream of your gateway, not downstream.

```
Applications → [Your gateway] → Marketplace → Orchestrators
```

There is currently no gateway layer in the marketplace. On-chain gateway registration (equivalent to how orchestrators register via ServiceRegistry) is on the roadmap but not yet available.

## For video gateways: the `-serviceAddr` in context

Video gateways use `-serviceAddr` differently from AI gateways. For a video gateway, `-serviceAddr` is the address that the gateway advertises to orchestrators so they can report results back. It must be reachable by orchestrators on the network.

This means your video gateway's service address must be publicly reachable on the gateway's HTTP port (default 8935). If you are behind NAT, configure port forwarding or use a server with a public IP.

RTMP ingest (port 1935) is separate from the service address and is what your video sources connect to. These can be on different ports or even different network interfaces.

## What is planned

The ecosystem is working toward more formal gateway discoverability:

- **On-chain gateway registry**: equivalent to the orchestrator ServiceRegistry, allowing gateways to register capabilities and be discovered programmatically
- **AI capability registry** (`-aiServiceRegistry`): a flag that already exists in go-livepeer for an AI-specific service registry — the infrastructure for AI gateway registration is partially in place
