Gateways pay orchestrators in ETH for every job completed — but not with a separate blockchain transaction per segment. At scale, that would be thousands of transactions per hour, each with gas overhead that would dwarf the payment itself. Livepeer solves this with **probabilistic micropayments (PM)**: a payment system where gateways send lightweight signed tickets, a fraction of which are "winners" redeemable for a larger ETH payout.

This page explains the full payment lifecycle: how tickets flow, what makes one a winner, how the Redeemer submits them on-chain, and what you need to keep your node funded and earning.

---

## Payment flow overview

```
Gateway routes job to your orchestrator
       ↓
You process the job (transcoding segment or AI inference)
       ↓
Gateway attaches a signed payment ticket to the job confirmation
       ↓
Your node evaluates the ticket — is it a winner?
       ↓
Non-winning tickets are discarded
Winning tickets are queued for on-chain submission
       ↓
The Redeemer submits winning tickets to the Arbitrum contract
       ↓
ETH credited to your orchestrator wallet on Arbitrum
       ↓
Your feeShare % flows to your delegators' claimable balance
```

Every step from ticket receipt to on-chain submission happens automatically inside go-livepeer. You don't manually trigger redemptions — you just keep your node running and your ETH balance sufficient for gas.

## Probabilistic micropayments explained

The core idea: instead of paying a tiny amount for every segment, the gateway sends tickets with a small **win probability**. When a ticket wins, it's worth a larger amount. Over many jobs, the expected value received equals what would have been paid if every single job had its own transaction.

```
Win probability:     e.g. 1 in 1000
Face value (if won): e.g. 1000 × the per-segment fee
Expected value:      1/1000 × 1000 × fee = fee per segment
```

This means:
- **Most tickets don't result in any on-chain action** — the Redeemer only submits winners
- **Winning tickets are infrequent but larger** — each represents many segments' worth of payment
- **Gas costs are batched implicitly** — one redemption transaction covers hundreds or thousands of segments

The result is that a busy orchestrator may redeem ETH a few times per day rather than thousands of times, keeping gas overhead negligible relative to earnings.

<Note>
  From a practical perspective, the probabilistic system means your ETH balance does not increase incrementally every minute. It increases in larger steps whenever a winning ticket is redeemed. This is expected behaviour — not a sign that something is broken.
</Note>

### Ticket structure

Each payment ticket the gateway sends contains:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| **Face value** | The ETH amount payable if this ticket wins |
| **Win probability** | The probability this ticket is a winner (e.g. `1e-5`) |
| **Sender address** | The gateway's Ethereum address |
| **Recipient address** | Your orchestrator's Ethereum address |
| **Sender deposit** | The gateway's on-chain deposit that backs all their tickets |
| **Expiry** | Block number after which this ticket cannot be redeemed |
| **Signature** | Gateway's Ethereum signature authorising payment |

Your node verifies the signature, checks the sender has sufficient deposit on-chain to back the ticket, and then evaluates the win condition using a deterministic random function. If the ticket wins, it goes to the Redeemer queue.

## The Redeemer

The Redeemer is a component of go-livepeer responsible for submitting winning tickets to the Arbitrum smart contract.

In a standard single-node setup, the Redeemer runs as part of your orchestrator process — you don't need to configure it separately. For multi-node or high-volume setups, the Redeemer can run as a separate service to centralise on-chain redemption across multiple worker nodes.

### What the Redeemer does

```
Winning ticket received from PM evaluator
       ↓
Redeemer batches pending winning tickets
       ↓
Redeemer submits batch transaction to Arbitrum PM contract
       ↓
Contract verifies: valid signature, sufficient sender deposit, not expired
       ↓
ETH transferred from gateway's deposit to your wallet
```

### Gas requirements for redemption

Redemption transactions run on Arbitrum One (L2). Gas costs are low, but your wallet must hold ETH on Arbitrum:

| Factor | Value |
|---|---|
| Gas per redemption transaction | ~100,000 – 200,000 gas |
| Typical Arbitrum gas price | 0.01 – 0.1 gwei |
| Approximate ETH cost per redemption | ~$0.01 – $0.05 USD |

Keep a minimum of **0.01 ETH on Arbitrum** in your orchestrator wallet to prevent redemption failures. A depleted wallet means winning tickets expire unsubmitted — that ETH is permanently lost.

<Warning>
  If your orchestrator wallet runs out of ETH on Arbitrum, winning tickets will fail to redeem and expire. The gateway's deposit is not affected — the loss is entirely on your side. Monitor your ETH balance and top it up regularly. See [Funding Your Node](#funding-your-node) below.
</Warning>

## Video vs AI payment differences

The probabilistic payment system is the same for both workload types, but the fee structure differs:

<AccordionGroup>

<Accordion title="Video transcoding payments">

Transcoding is paid per segment — approximately every 2 seconds of video processed.

- **Pricing unit:** per segment (or per pixel, depending on configuration)
- **Job frequency:** continuous for active live streams
- **Payment trigger:** one ticket per segment delivered
- **Selection factor:** stake-weighted routing; higher total stake = more segments routed to you
- **Typical fee level:** low per-segment (highly competitive, commoditised pipeline)

The transcoding fee rate is set by your `-pricePerUnit` flag in Wei per pixel. Gateways filter orchestrators by maximum acceptable price before routing.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="AI inference payments">

AI inference is paid per inference job — one ticket per job request.

- **Pricing unit:** per output pixel (image pipelines), per ms of audio (audio-to-text), per token (LLM), or per frame (live video)
- **Job frequency:** per-request (not continuous)
- **Payment trigger:** one ticket per completed inference job
- **Selection factor:** capability first, price second; stake weight is a secondary factor
- **Typical fee level:** higher per-job than transcoding (GPU-intensive work)

AI fees are configured in `aiModels.json` via the `price_per_unit` field. The pricing unit varies by pipeline — see [Model and VRAM Reference](/v2/orchestrators/guides/workloads/model-vram-reference) for per-pipeline pricing units.

</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Live AI (Cascade) payments">

Real-time video AI (live-video-to-video) uses a time-based payment model rather than per-job.

- **Pricing unit:** per second of active stream
- **Payment trigger:** periodic PM tickets at regular intervals during the stream session
- **Payment state:** the gateway maintains running session state to calculate fees based on elapsed time
- **Relevance to remote signers:** Live AI payment state is managed by the remote signer component (PRs #3791 and #3822). See [Remote Signers](/v2/gateways/payments/remote-signers) for gateway-side context.

</Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>

## Clearinghouses

In the standard PM model, the gateway itself holds an Ethereum deposit on-chain and signs payment tickets directly. A **clearinghouse** is an alternative arrangement where a third-party operator manages the PM deposit and ticket signing on behalf of gateways that don't want to handle crypto infrastructure directly.

From the orchestrator's perspective, payments from a gateway using a clearinghouse are identical to direct PM payments. The same ticket structure, the same Redeemer process, the same Arbitrum redemption.

Clearinghouses exist to enable:
- Gateway implementations on platforms that can't easily handle Ethereum keys (browser, mobile)
- Enterprise gateways that want to settle in fiat while still using Livepeer orchestrators
- Separation of media processing concerns from payment logistics

This is a gateway-side concern. As an orchestrator, you receive valid signed tickets and redeem them — the clearinghouse arrangement is transparent to you.

For the gateway-side detail, see [Payment Clearinghouses](/v2/gateways/payments/payment-clearinghouse).

## Funding your node

Your orchestrator wallet on Arbitrum needs ETH for three types of transaction:

| Transaction type | Approximate ETH needed | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Reward() call | ~0.000005 – 0.00005 ETH per round | Once per round (~22h) |
| Ticket redemption | ~0.00002 – 0.0001 ETH per batch | Multiple times daily at volume |
| Activation / re-registration | ~0.0001 ETH one-time | Rare |

**Recommended minimum balance:** 0.01 ETH on Arbitrum. Replenish when you fall below 0.005 ETH.

### Bridging ETH to Arbitrum

If you hold ETH on Ethereum mainnet and need to move it to Arbitrum One:

<Steps>

<Step title="Use the Arbitrum official bridge">
  Go to [bridge.arbitrum.io](https://bridge.arbitrum.io) and connect the wallet corresponding to your orchestrator address.
</Step>

<Step title="Bridge ETH from L1 to Arbitrum One">
  Select Ethereum → Arbitrum One. Enter the amount to bridge. Bridging takes approximately 15 minutes for the deposit to confirm on Arbitrum.
</Step>

<Step title="Verify receipt on Arbitrum">
  Check [arbiscan.io](https://arbiscan.io) for your orchestrator address to confirm the ETH arrived.
</Step>

<Step title="Set up balance monitoring">
  Add your orchestrator wallet address to a balance monitoring tool. Alert when balance drops below your threshold. Most operators use Prometheus + Grafana for this — see [Metrics and Monitoring](/v2/orchestrators/guides/monitoring/metrics).
</Step>

</Steps>

ETH is also available on Arbitrum directly from centralised exchanges that support Arbitrum One withdrawals (Binance, Coinbase, Kraken). Check current exchange support before withdrawing.

## Checking payment health

The go-livepeer node exposes Prometheus metrics for payment activity. Key ones to watch:

| Metric | What it shows |
|---|---|
| `livepeer_pm_winning_tickets_received` | Winning tickets your node has validated |
| `livepeer_pm_winning_tickets_redeemed` | Tickets successfully submitted on-chain |
| `livepeer_pm_ticket_redemption_errors` | Failed redemptions (often ETH balance or network issues) |
| `livepeer_eth_balance` | Your current orchestrator ETH balance on Arbitrum |

A healthy node has `tickets_redeemed` tracking closely behind `tickets_received`, and zero sustained `redemption_errors`. Persistent errors with `redemption_errors > 0` usually indicate a depleted ETH balance or a connectivity issue with your Arbitrum RPC endpoint.

For the complete Prometheus metric reference, see [Metrics and Monitoring](/v2/orchestrators/guides/monitoring/metrics).
