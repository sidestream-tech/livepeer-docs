### Technical Role
An Orchestrator is a supply-side operator that contributes compute resources (predominately GPU resources) to the network.
They receive jobs, perform transcoding or AI inference, and get compensated via LPT rewards + ETH fees.

This makes them a critical part of the network's ecosystem and a key partner for developers building on top of Livepeer.

See <LinkArrow href="/v2/orchestrators/concepts/capabilities" label="Capabilities and Workloads" newline={false} /> for more on the types of workloads orchestrators run.
### Network Security Role
Orchestrators are also economic actors that stake LPT to secure the network and earn rewards.
They manage their own staking and rewards, vote in governance proposals, and can influence the direction of the network via their delegate power.

See <LinkArrow href="/v2/orchestrators/guides/staking-and-rewards" label="Staking and Rewards" newline={false} /> for more on how orchestrators manage their stake, earn rewards, and participate in governance.

### Governance Role
Orchestrators are not just passive compute providers; they are active participants in the Livepeer Network.
They can have significant influence on the direction of the network via their delegate power and votes on governance proposals.

See <LinkArrow href="/v2/orchestrators/guides/delegates-voting-pools" label="Delegates & Voting" newline={false} /> for more on how orchestrators can engage with and influence the network's governance processes.

### Business Role
Orchestrators are key partners for developers building on top of Livepeer, as they provide the compute resources that power real-time video and AI workloads across the network.
Running an Orchestrator and partnering with application developers enables operators to earn further rewards and revenue by providing the necessary compute resources for these applications to function effectively.

### Orchestrator Setup Paths
There are multiple ways to get started as an orchestrator on the Livepeer Network, depending on your resources, goals, and experience level. You can contribute your GPU resources to an existing Orchestrator pool for a more passive experience, or you can set up your own Orchestrator node for full control and potentially higher rewards.
- See [Add your consumer GPU to a pool](../get-started/join-a-pool): Best for hobbyists or those with limited technical expertise
- See [Run your own Orchestrator node](../setup): For those who want full control over their node and are comfortable with the technical requirements.

### Orchestrator Earnings & Staking Requirements
In order to earn rewards as an orchestrator, you need to stake LPT and run a node that performs work for the network.

See [Staking Requirements](v2/orchestrators/guides/staking-and-rewards/rcs-staking-lpt) for the current minimum staking requirements to run an orchestrator node on the Livepeer Network.

Orchestrators earn rewards through a combination of LPT rewards and ETH fees.
The amount they earn depends on several factors, including the type of workload they run (video transcoding vs AI inference), the performance of their node, and the demand for their services on the network.

See [Orchestrator Earnings Explained](/v2/orchestrators/operations/earnings) for a detailed breakdown of how orchestrators earn and the factors that influence their earning potential.

### Setup Options
Setting up an Orchestrator means installing the necessary software, connecting to the network, and activating your node.
- For a straightforward setup, you can use the [Go-Livepeer CLI](/v2/orchestrators/setup/guide) which provides a streamlined installation and activation process.
- For more customized setups, you can follow the [Alternate Setups Guide](/v2/orchestrators/guides/setups-and-workloads/rc-alternate-setups) which covers different hardware configurations, workload optimizations, and operational strategies based on your specific goals and resources. */}
