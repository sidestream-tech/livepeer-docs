## Workload types

Orchestrators earn ETH service fees by completing jobs routed to them by gateways. Four categories of compute work are available on the network today.

### Video transcoding

The original workload on Livepeer, live since 2017. Gateways send RTMP video segments; your node re-encodes them into the requested output profiles (resolution, bitrate, format) and returns the results. Transcoding can run on CPU alone, but NVIDIA GPU acceleration (NVENC/NVDEC) is strongly recommended to remain competitive.

### Batch AI inference

Added in Q3 2024 with the AI Subnet launch. Your node receives an AI request (text-to-image, image-to-video, audio-to-text, etc.), runs inference via an `ai-runner` Docker container, and returns the result. Nine distinct pipelines are currently supported, with varying VRAM requirements.

### Real-time AI (Cascade)

Architecturally distinct from batch inference. Instead of one request = one response, your GPU is allocated to a **persistent live video stream**, processing each frame through a ComfyUI workflow in real time. Used for live avatar generation, style transfer overlays, and interactive AI applications.

### LLM inference

Text completion and chat using large language models. Requires a separate Ollama-based runner container. Quantised 7-8B models fit in 8GB VRAM, making this accessible to operators with older consumer GPUs who cannot run the diffusion-based image pipelines.

### BYOC (Bring Your Own Container)

Applications can define custom container-based workloads that your orchestrator executes. This extends the network beyond standard AI pipelines into enterprise-specific processing, custom ML workflows, and novel applications like <LinkArrow href="/v2/solutions/embody" label="Embody AI avatars" newline={false} /> and <LinkArrow href="/v2/solutions/daydream" label="Daydream real-time AI video" newline={false} />.

## Boundary: orchestrators vs gateways

Orchestrators provide compute. They do **not**:

- Route or schedule jobs (gateways do this)
- Manage customer relationships, API keys, or billing (gateways or application layer)
- Set business pricing for end users (gateways set margins)
- Ingest video streams directly from applications (gateways handle ingest)

The gateway is the demand side; the orchestrator is the supply side. If you want to route work rather than compute it, see <LinkArrow href="/v2/gateways/concepts/role" label="Gateway Role" newline={false} />.

## Beyond compute

Orchestrators are not passive compute providers. They are active protocol participants with responsibilities and influence beyond processing jobs.

### Staking and delegation

Orchestrators stake LPT to register on the network and attract delegation from LPT holders. Your total stake (self-stake + delegated stake) determines:

- Whether you are in the **active set** (eligible for work and rewards)
- Your **selection probability** (gateways factor stake weight into orchestrator ranking)
- Your **protocol reward share** (proportional to stake)

You configure **reward cut** and **fee cut** percentages to determine how rewards and fees are split with your delegators. See <LinkArrow href="/v2/orchestrators/guides/staking-and-rewards" label="Staking and Rewards" newline={false} />.

### Governance

Orchestrators vote on **Livepeer Improvement Proposals (LIPs)** that change protocol parameters, add new features, or alter network economics. Your voting weight is proportional to your total stake, making high-stake orchestrators influential governance actors.

See <LinkArrow href="/v2/orchestrators/guides/delegates-voting-pools" label="Delegates and Voting" newline={false} />.

### Reward calls

Once per round (~24 hours), your node calls `reward()` on the Arbitrum smart contracts to mint your share of newly created LPT. Missing a reward call forfeits that round's rewards for you and your delegators.

## What should you run?

The workloads you can offer depend on your hardware and goals.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Consumer GPU (8-12GB)">
    - **Transcoding** - always available, no VRAM minimum
    - **LLM inference** - quantised 7-8B models fit in 8GB
    - **Lightweight AI** - `image-to-text` (4GB), `segment-anything-2` (6GB)

    Best for: hobbyists, passive income, older GPUs
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Mid-range GPU (16GB)">
    - Everything above, plus:
    - **Batch AI** - audio-to-text, image-to-image, and more pipelines
    - **Real-time AI** - lightweight ComfyStream workflows

    Best for: serious operators entering the AI subnet
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="High-end GPU (24GB+)">
    - Everything above, plus:
    - **Full AI suite** - text-to-image (SDXL), image-to-video
    - **Production real-time AI** - style transfer, avatar workflows

    Best for: data centres, dedicated AI operators, maximum revenue potential
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

See <LinkArrow href="/v2/orchestrators/concepts/rs-workloads" label="Job Types" newline={false} /> for per-pipeline hardware requirements and pricing configuration.

## Example services powered by orchestrators

Real applications running on orchestrator compute today:

- **[Daydream](/v2/solutions/daydream)** - interactive AI video platform powering real-time AI experiences
- **[Embody](/v2/solutions/embody)** - AI avatar generation for virtual agents and VTubers
- **Video streaming** - live transcoding for platforms using Livepeer as their encoding backend
- **BYOC workloads** - custom ML pipelines and enterprise processing
