Choosing an orchestrator is the most consequential decision you make as a delegator. A well-run orchestrator with consistent reward calls and fair commission settings earns you meaningful returns. A poorly run one — even one with a 0% commission — can earn you nothing if they never call `Reward()`.

This guide walks you through how to evaluate orchestrators and ends with the actual delegation transaction steps.

<Info>
  **Network:** LPT staking runs on **Arbitrum One**. If your LPT is on Ethereum mainnet, bridge it before starting. Use the [Arbitrum Bridge](https://bridge.arbitrum.io) or the [Livepeer L2 Migration Tool](https://explorer.livepeer.org/migrate).
</Info>

---

## Researched delegation vs blind delegation

| | Researched delegation | Blind delegation (just picking a low commission) |
|---|---|---|
| Commission rate | Verified and contextualised | Taken at face value |
| Reward call history | Checked over 30+ rounds | Unknown |
| Active set status | Confirmed | Assumed |
| Stake concentration | Considered | Ignored |
| Expected return | Estimated with reasonable confidence | Unpredictable |
| Time to evaluate | 15 minutes | 2 minutes |

Those 13 extra minutes are worth it. An orchestrator with a 5% rewardCut who misses one in three reward calls delivers worse returns than one with a 10% rewardCut who calls `Reward()` every single round.

You need:
- LPT on Arbitrum One (not Ethereum mainnet)
- A small amount of ETH on Arbitrum One for gas (Arbitrum transactions cost a few cents)
- A compatible wallet: MetaMask, Coinbase Wallet, or any WalletConnect-compatible wallet

## Step 1: Open the Livepeer Explorer

Navigate to [explorer.livepeer.org](https://explorer.livepeer.org) and connect your wallet.

The main orchestrators page shows all registered orchestrators, sortable by stake, reward rate, and fee cut. You are looking at the full list — not just the top 100. The top 100 by bonded stake form the **active set**: the only orchestrators eligible to earn rewards in the current round.

<Tip>
  Filter or sort by "Active" first. Delegating to an inactive orchestrator means your stake contributes to their potential activation but earns nothing until they enter the active set.
</Tip>

### What the active set means

The Livepeer protocol selects the top 100 orchestrators by total bonded stake at the start of each round. Only these 100 earn inflation rewards or are routed video and AI jobs that round. Orchestrators outside the top 100 earn nothing regardless of their commission settings or uptime.

When you delegate, you want your orchestrator to be firmly in the active set — not borderline — so that changes in other orchestrators' stake do not knock them out between rounds.

## Step 2: Check the reward call history

This is the single most important signal. Before you look at commission rates, find out how reliably the orchestrator calls `Reward()` each round.

On the orchestrator's Explorer profile page, look for their reward call history section. A healthy orchestrator calls `Reward()` in every single round they are active.

### What you are looking for

**Strong signal:** Called reward 30 of last 30 rounds (or close to it)

**Acceptable:** Occasional missed rounds due to temporary downtime — but look for a pattern of recovery and consistency, not ongoing gaps

**Reject:** Called reward 15 of last 30 rounds, or multiple consecutive missed rounds, or a recent long streak of inactivity

<AccordionGroup>

<Accordion title="Why missed reward calls hurt you more than commission">
  Each missed reward call means **zero LPT inflation is distributed for that round** to any delegator with that orchestrator. If an orchestrator misses 10 out of 30 rounds, you are getting roughly one-third fewer inflation rewards than the headline yield figure suggests. A 0% rewardCut orchestrator who calls reward 20 of 30 rounds delivers the same effective yield as a 33% rewardCut orchestrator who calls every round.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Why do orchestrators miss reward calls?">
  Reward calls require a small ETH balance to pay Arbitrum gas. An orchestrator that lets their ETH balance run to zero will stop calling `Reward()` until they top up. Other causes include node downtime, misconfiguration after an update, or an operator going inactive without formally leaving the network. The pattern matters: a one-off miss is different from a structural problem.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Does an uncalled round mean the rewards are lost forever?">
  Yes. If an orchestrator does not call `Reward()` in a round, the inflation that would have been minted for that round's stake is simply not minted. It cannot be recovered retroactively by calling in the next round.
</Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>

## Step 3: Evaluate commission settings

With a reliable reward-calling track record confirmed, now look at the commission settings.

### rewardCut (LPT inflation commission)

The percentage of LPT inflation rewards the orchestrator keeps. The remainder is distributed to delegators.

- 0% rewardCut: orchestrator keeps nothing, delegators receive 100% of their proportional share
- 10% rewardCut: orchestrator keeps 10%, delegators receive 90%
- 50% rewardCut: delegators receive only half the inflation they would otherwise earn

**Lower rewardCut is better for delegators.** Most competitive orchestrators sit between 0% and 20%.

### feeShare (ETH fee distribution)

The percentage of ETH fee revenue the orchestrator passes to delegators. This appears as "Fee Share" in the Explorer.

- 80% feeShare: delegators receive 80% of all ETH fees the orchestrator earns
- 25% feeShare: delegators receive only 25% of ETH fees

**Higher feeShare is better for delegators.**

<Warning>
  These two parameters move in opposite directions. For rewardCut, lower is better. For feeShare, higher is better. Read both before deciding — do not rely on one alone.
</Warning>

### Commission changes after you delegate

Orchestrators can change their commission settings at any time, effective from the next round. There is no minimum lock-in from their side. Some orchestrators attract delegators with low rates and then raise them once they have sufficient stake. Check the orchestrator's commission history if the Explorer exposes it, and monitor your position periodically after delegating.

---

## Step 4: Check stake concentration

Look at the orchestrator's total bonded stake relative to the network.

**Why this matters:** an orchestrator with a very large share of total network stake earns proportionally more from each round, but you share those earnings with a proportionally larger pool of delegators. Your individual return stays the same in theory, but you are also contributing to centralisation.

From a network health perspective, delegating to smaller, reliable orchestrators is better for decentralisation and long-term protocol security. From a pure-yield perspective, the maths are the same if the orchestrator calls `Reward()` consistently.

**Practical heuristic:**
- Orchestrators outside the top 10 by stake are generally a better choice for network health
- Avoid orchestrators who are only just inside the active set threshold — one bad round for their delegators could push them out

## Step 5: Consider alignment and longevity

Optional but worth a few minutes for anyone delegating meaningful amounts.

<AccordionGroup>

<Accordion title="How long has the orchestrator been active?">
  A node that has been in the active set for 12+ months with consistent reward calls is a much lower-risk choice than one that launched last month. Check their activation date on Explorer.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Do they have a public identity and community presence?">
  Some orchestrators are anonymous, some operate under a brand name, some are run by well-known Livepeer community contributors (such as Titan Node or other community SPEs). A public identity does not guarantee performance, but it does mean they have reputational skin in the game.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Have they participated in governance?">
  On-chain voting history is visible on the Explorer. Orchestrators who participate in governance votes are typically more invested in the protocol's long-term health. This matters more if you care about the governance value of your bonded stake.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Do they have an active Discord or social presence?">
  Some orchestrators post update notices in the Livepeer Discord [#orchestrators](https://discord.gg/livepeer) channel. This matters if you want to know about planned maintenance windows or commission changes before they happen.
</Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>

## Orchestrator selection: summary checklist

Before moving to the delegation step, confirm:

- [ ] Orchestrator is in the active set (top 100 by bonded stake)
- [ ] Reward call history: 28+ of last 30 rounds called (or equivalent consistency)
- [ ] rewardCut is competitive (typically below 20%)
- [ ] feeShare is meaningful (above 50% if ETH fees matter to you)
- [ ] Orchestrator has been active for a reasonable period
- [ ] Stake share is not so large that they dominate the network

### The delegation transaction in detail

<Steps>

<Step title="Go to the orchestrator's profile page">
  In the [Livepeer Explorer](https://explorer.livepeer.org), click on the orchestrator you have chosen. Their profile shows current stats, commission settings, and reward history.

  Confirm their status shows **Active** before proceeding.
</Step>

<Step title="Click Delegate">
  On the orchestrator's profile page, click the **Delegate** button. Enter the amount of LPT you want to bond.

  You do not have to delegate your full balance. You can delegate a portion and keep the rest liquid, though unbonded LPT earns nothing and is being diluted by inflation.

  <Tip>
    Start with a smaller amount if you are new to delegation. You can always delegate more later without unbonding — just call `bond()` again to add to your existing position.
  </Tip>
</Step>

<Step title="Sign the Approve transaction">
  The first transaction authorises the BondingManager smart contract to transfer your LPT. This is a standard ERC-20 approval and does not move any tokens yet.

  **Gas cost:** a few cents on Arbitrum One.

  Wait for this transaction to confirm before proceeding.
</Step>

<Step title="Sign the Bond transaction">
  The second transaction executes the delegation. It transfers your LPT into the BondingManager contract and attributes your stake to the orchestrator you chose.

  **Gas cost:** a few cents on Arbitrum One.

  Wait for this transaction to confirm.
</Step>

<Step title="Verify your delegation">
  After the Bond transaction confirms, your delegation is live. In the Explorer, navigate to your account page (connected wallet address). You should see:

  - Your bonded balance
  - The orchestrator address your stake is attributed to
  - Your pending rewards (initially zero, increases each round)

  You can also verify on-chain directly via Arbiscan by reading your address's state in the BondingManager contract.
</Step>

</Steps>

## What happens next

Your stake is now bonded. From this round forward, your orchestrator earns slightly more work and rewards because your stake increases their weight in the network.

Rewards accumulate automatically each round when your orchestrator calls `Reward()`. You do not need to do anything for rewards to accrue — but you will need to claim them to compound or withdraw. See [Manage Your Delegation](./manage-your-delegation) for the next steps.

## Frequently asked questions

<AccordionGroup>

<Accordion title="Can I delegate to more than one orchestrator?">
  Not from a single address. Your bonded stake is attributed to exactly one orchestrator at a time. To diversify across orchestrators, you would need separate wallets with separate LPT balances.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What if my orchestrator leaves the active set after I delegate?">
  Your stake remains bonded to them, but you earn no inflation rewards in rounds where they are inactive. The moment you notice this, redelegate to an active orchestrator. Redelegation (changing your delegate without unbonding) takes effect in the next round and does not require waiting through an unbonding period.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Can I add more LPT to my existing delegation?">
  Yes. Call `bond()` again from the same wallet to add LPT to your existing bonded position. You do not need to unbond first. Your new amount is added to your current bonded balance and attributed to the same orchestrator.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Can I change orchestrators without unbonding?">
  Yes. You can redelegate to a different orchestrator at any time. This moves your stake attribution to the new orchestrator immediately (effective next round) without going through the unbonding waiting period. Your bonded balance stays in the contract — only the attribution changes.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How much ETH do I need for gas?">
  The approval and bond transactions together cost well under $0.10 on Arbitrum One under normal conditions. Keep a small ETH balance (0.001 ETH is more than enough) for the two transactions and for future claims or redelegation.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="I delegated but see no rewards yet — is something wrong?">
  Rewards accrue each round when your orchestrator calls `Reward()`. If you delegated mid-round, you may need to wait until the following round to see your first accrual. If you see no rewards after 2–3 rounds, check your orchestrator's reward call history on the Explorer to confirm they are calling `Reward()` consistently.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Is there any risk of losing my LPT?">
  The primary risk is opportunity cost — if your orchestrator stops calling `Reward()`, you earn nothing while your stake remains locked. The slashing mechanism (which would reduce your bonded balance as a penalty) exists in the protocol but is currently disabled on mainnet. Your LPT cannot be seized by the orchestrator.
</Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>
